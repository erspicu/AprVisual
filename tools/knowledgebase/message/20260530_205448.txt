Here is a rigorously checked list of canonical citations for your academic credits section. I have verified the titles, authors, years, and venues. 

### 1. Switch-Level MOS Simulation Model
*   **Bryant (1984):** Randal E. Bryant, *"A Switch-Level Model and Simulator for MOS Digital Systems"*, IEEE Transactions on Computers, Vol. C-33, No. 2, pp. 160–177, 1984. *(Note: Originates from his 1981 MIT Ph.D. thesis, "A Switch-Level Simulation Model for Integrated Logic Circuits").*
*   **What it is:** Models MOS circuits as networks of bidirectional switches and multi-strength nodes evaluated via relaxation to a steady state.
*   **Confidence:** High.

### 2. Cuthill–McKee and Reverse Cuthill–McKee (RCM)
*   **Cuthill & McKee (1969):** E. Cuthill and J. McKee, *"Reducing the Bandwidth of Sparse Symmetric Matrices"*, Proceedings of the 24th National Conference (ACM '69), 1969.
*   **George (1971):** J. Alan George, *"Computer Implementation of the Finite Element Method"*, Stanford University Tech Report STAN-CS-71-208, 1971. *(Introduced the "Reverse" variant).*
*   **What it is:** A graph/matrix reordering algorithm that relabels nodes to keep connected neighbors close in memory, drastically reducing bandwidth and improving cache locality.
*   **Confidence:** High.

### 3. Direction-Optimizing BFS & Ligra Framework
*   **Beamer et al. (2012):** Scott Beamer, Krste Asanović, and David Patterson, *"Direction-Optimizing Breadth-First Search"*, Proceedings of the International Conference on High Performance Computing, Networking, Storage and Analysis (SC '12), 2012.
*   **Shun & Blelloch (2013):** Julian Shun and Guy E. Blelloch, *"Ligra: A Lightweight Graph Processing Framework for Shared Memory"*, Proceedings of the 18th ACM SIGPLAN Symposium on Principles and Practice of Parallel Programming (PPoPP '13), 2013.
*   **What it is:** A traversal method that dynamically switches between top-down (push) and bottom-up (pull) execution based on frontier density to avoid redundant edge checks.
*   **Confidence:** High.

### 4. Union-Find / Disjoint-Set (with Path Compression)
*   **Galler & Fisher (1964):** Bernard A. Galler and Michael J. Fisher, *"An Improved Equivalence Algorithm"*, Communications of the ACM, Vol. 7, No. 5, 1964. *(Initial tree-based data structure).*
*   **Tarjan (1975):** Robert E. Tarjan, *"Efficiency of a Good But Not Linear Set Union Algorithm"*, Journal of the ACM (JACM), Vol. 22, No. 2, 1975. *(Proved the inverse Ackermann time bound for path compression + union-by-rank).*
*   **What it is:** A data structure that tracks partitioned sets (used to merge always-on shorts) with near-constant time operations via tree flattening.
*   **Confidence:** High.

### 5. Strongly Connected Components (SCC)
*   **Tarjan (1972):** Robert E. Tarjan, *"Depth-First Search and Linear Graph Algorithms"*, SIAM Journal on Computing, Vol. 1, No. 2, 1972.
*   **What it is:** A definitive, single-pass depth-first search algorithm to identify cycles/strongly connected components in directed graphs in linear time.
*   **Confidence:** High. *(Kosaraju's algorithm is also classic, published by Sharir in 1981, but Tarjan 1972 is the definitive first publication of linear-time SCC).*

### 6. Worklist / Event-Driven Fixpoint Iteration
*   **Kildall (1973):** Gary A. Kildall, *"A Unified Approach to Global Program Optimization"*, Proceedings of the 1st Annual ACM SIGACT-SIGPLAN Symposium on Principles of Programming Languages (POPL '73), 1973.
*   **What it is:** The seminal formalization of iterative dataflow analysis using a worklist (queue of active nodes) to evaluate state changes until a fixpoint (steady state) is reached.
*   **Confidence:** High. 

### 7. Data-Oriented Design / Structure-of-Arrays (SoA)
*   **Acton (2014):** Mike Acton, *"Data-Oriented Design and C++"*, CppCon Keynote, 2014. *(Canonical modern systems/gaming reference).*
*   **Hunt (1989):** D. J. Hunt, *"AMT DAP - a processor array in a workstation environment"*, Computer Systems Science and Engineering, Vol. 4, No. 2, 1989. *(Academic roots of SoA for SIMD/Vector computing).*
*   **What it is:** Organizing data layouts by fields (arrays of components) rather than objects to maximize contiguous memory loads and utilize CPU caches efficiently.
*   **Confidence:** High for the references, though "DoD" as a named concept stems from industry (Llopis/Acton) rather than a single academic paper.

### 8. The Memory Wall
*   **Wulf & McKee (1995):** Wm. A. Wulf and Sally A. McKee, *"Hitting the Memory Wall: Implications of the Obvious"*, ACM SIGARCH Computer Architecture News, Vol. 23, No. 1, 1995.
*   **What it is:** The observation that microprocessor logic scales exponentially faster than DRAM latency, making memory access the primary bottleneck in modern computing.
*   **Confidence:** High.

### 9. Profile-Guided Optimization (PGO)
*   **Pettis & Hansen (1990):** Karl Pettis and Robert C. Hansen, *"Profile Guided Code Positioning"*, Proceedings of the ACM SIGPLAN Conference on Programming Language Design and Implementation (PLDI '90), 1990.
*   **What it is:** A compiler optimization technique that uses execution data to reorder basic blocks and split functions, dramatically improving instruction cache hit rates.
*   **Confidence:** High. *(This is the canonical paper for block-level PGO).*

### 10. Bit-Parallel / Bitset Breadth-First Traversal
*   **Arlazarov et al. (1970):** V. L. Arlazarov, E. A. Dinic, M. A. Kronrod, and I. A. Faradzev, *"On Economical Construction of the Transitive Closure of a Directed Graph"*, Doklady Akademii Nauk SSSR, Vol. 194, 1970. *(Commonly known as the "Method of Four Russians").*
*   **What it is:** Packing boolean graph adjacency and frontier states into machine words to process multiple neighbors simultaneously via bitwise instructions.
*   **Confidence:** Medium. *(While "Four Russians" is the seminal origin of using bit-packed machine words to accelerate boolean graph traversal/transitive closure, there is no single canonical paper exclusively titled "Bitset BFS"; Four Russians is the academically correct root to cite).*

### Additional Classic References to Consider
If you want to historically ground your simulator, these three papers track the evolution of circuit simulation from pure analog, to digital timing, to switch-level:

*   **Analog Baseline (SPICE):** L. W. Nagel and D. O. Pederson, *"SPICE (Simulation Program with Integrated Circuit Emphasis)"*, University of California, Berkeley, UCB/ERL M382, 1973. *(The genesis of modern continuous-time transistor simulation).*
*   **Timing Simulation (MOTIS):** B. R. Chawla, H. K. Gummel, and P. Kozak, *"MOTIS - An MOS Timing Simulator"*, IEEE Transactions on Circuits and Systems, 1975. *(The first bridge between continuous analog simulation and discrete digital logic).*
*   **RC Switch-Level (RSIM):** Christopher J. Terman, *"RSIM - A Logic-Level Timing Simulator"*, Proceedings of the 20th Design Automation Conference (DAC '83), 1983. *(Extended Bryant's MOSSIM switch-level approach to compute realistic RC delay estimates without full analog overhead).*
