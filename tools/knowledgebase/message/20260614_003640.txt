**6. BRUTAL BOTTOM LINE (Answering this first to frame the rest):**
**STOP.** Multi-core acceleration of this single-instance, bit-exact, switch-level workload is dead. You have a theoretical Amdahl ceiling of 1.20x, and you are trying to squeeze it out of an architecture where the synchronization overhead is an order of magnitude larger than the work itself. Even if you executed perfectly on the coarse-grained path, you *might* achieve a 1.05x speedup. You are 316x away from real-time. A 5% gain for massive architectural complexity is a total waste of time. Embarrassingly parallel throughput (e.g., genetic algorithms, fuzzing, or hosting multiple independent game instances) is the *only* honest use of multiple cores for this engine. 

Here are the concrete answers to your specific questions:

**1. Are per-wave barriers dead? Any intermediate granularity?**
Yes, mathematically dead. Your per-wave work is ~700ns. Your sync is 134ns. Amdahl's law with a ~20% sync tax on a best-case 50/50 split means you can never scale.
There is no intermediate granularity. At the switch level, the clock edges define the boundaries of causality. The engine settles until quiescence within a half-cycle. You cannot sync mid-settle without violating the Gauss-Seidel semantics, and syncing per-node is obviously absurd. 

**2. Cross-core sync floor on Zen 2?**
134 ns is very close to the practical floor for a high-level language on this silicon.
If you pin to two cores *within the same CCX* (sharing the 16MB L3 cache), the hardware L2-to-L2 cache coherency ping-pong is roughly 40-50ns. If you drop to native C/assembly and use `MONITORX`/`MWAITX` to avoid atomic `LOCK` prefixes and cache-line bouncing, you might get the round-trip down to ~70ns. But in C#, standard `Volatile.Read`/`SpinWait` overheads will keep you exactly where you are. Even at 70ns, it is still fatal against your ~700ns of work.

**3. The coarse path and a "private clock tree" trick?**
Any attempt to branch in your hot loop (`if (node_id < SHARED_THRESHOLD) read_shared() else read_private()`) will introduce pipeline stalls and destroy the dependent-load-latency chain that your engine currently relies on. 
The only "clean" trick is virtual memory aliasing (mapping two different virtual addresses to the same physical page for shared nodes, and different pages for private nodes), but C# makes this virtually impossible without catastrophic unsafe P/Invoke overhead. 
Furthermore, duplicating the entire 15KB state per thread every half-cycle (the rollback/merge phase) means copying 15KB * 42.95M times per real-time second. That is ~645 GB/s of L1 bandwidth just for state copying. It will choke your CPU.

**4. Parallelizing observable Gauss-Seidel semantics?**
No. If the iteration order defines the result (chaotic relaxation), it is mathematically equivalent to a strict dependency chain. The only known computer science technique to parallelize a strict dependency chain is Speculative Execution (predict/execute/rollback). You already modeled this for the coarse path, and as you noted, the rollback overhead (memcpy) and conflict rate make it a net negative.

**5. Splitting the PPU SCC?**
You cannot spatially decompose a single strongly connected component at the transistor level without introducing "ghost nodes" (overlapping boundary states calculated redundantly by both threads to resolve boundary conflicts). Because the PPU is an unsplittable bidirectional SCC, the ghost-node overlap would approach the size of the PPU itself. You would end up doing 1.5x the total work just to split it in half, completely erasing any parallel advantage.

**Summary:**
You built a highly optimized, L1-resident, dependent-load-bound state machine. You have successfully hit the single-thread limit of the architecture. You cannot partition the un-partitionable. Take the win on the single-core optimization, drop the multi-threading pursuit for a single instance, and pivot to either pure algorithmic single-core optimizations or multi-instance workloads.
