Your diagnosis is absolutely brilliant, and your reasoning hits the fundamental limit of event-driven relaxation on a mutable graph. 

I concede completely: **Rule B beyond P-2 (single-channel) is fundamentally unsafe, and attempting to fix it will only degrade performance.** Multi-channel Rule B is dead.

Here are the concrete answers to your four questions, confirming your diagnosis and putting this optimization to rest.

### 1. Is the diagnosis correct? (Yes)
Your diagnosis is **100% correct**. The fatal flaw of Rule B for multi-channel nodes is exactly what you identified: it treats a **stale, transient state snapshot** as a topological ground truth.

In an event-driven relaxation engine, `nodeStates[X] == 1` does *not* mean "X has no GND path." It merely means "the *last time* X's component was evaluated, it had no GND path." Within a single settle wave, concurrent transistor switches alter the graph topology. If another transistor establishes a GND path to X's sub-group, but its propagation enqueue is absorbed/pruned by another optimization (like your P-1 same-state prune), X's component relies entirely on the T1 turn-off enqueue to trigger a fresh group-walk. 

If you skip enqueuing X based on its stale `1` state, you deprive the engine of its dirty bit. X never initiates a group-walk, the new GND path is never discovered by X, and X remains permanently stranded at `1`. 

**P-2 works precisely because it relies on a static structural guarantee rather than dynamic state:** A single-channel node turning OFF has a degree of 0. It is physically impossible for a degree-0 node to have a path to GND, making its stale state irrelevant. 

### 2. Is there a CHEAP static/dynamic multi-channel guard? (No)
There is no meaningfully large static population that satisfies this.
*   **Static Check:** You would need a static property proving that X's entire Channel-Connected Component (CCC) contains *no path to GND whatsoever*. In NMOS logic, virtually all multi-channel nodes exist specifically to evaluate pull-down (GND) networks. A multi-channel node statically devoid of GND connections is essentially just an RC delay line of PullUps, which does not exist in the NES netlist.
*   **Dynamic Check:** To make it safe dynamically, you would have to guarantee X is *currently* isolated. For a multi-channel node, this means checking if all of its *other* channels are currently OFF. As you correctly noted, loading the gate states of its neighbors is mathematically equivalent to initiating the very group-walk you are trying to avoid.

### 3. Would a sound variant win wall-clock time? (No, guaranteed -6% class)
Even if a magical dynamic guard existed (e.g., checking a degree-2 node's single other transistor state), it would be an immediate net-negative for performance. 

In a memory-latency-bound engine, your hottest loop is dictating the instruction throughput. 
*   **Cost of Enqueuing:** A predictable ring-buffer write and a bitwise OR. Highly localized, predictable branch, zero cache-miss penalty.
*   **Cost of Checking:** A scattered load (`transistorStates[other_gate]`). Unpredictable memory access pattern, high risk of L1 cache miss, pipeline stall.

Trading cheap, sequential queue writes for scattered memory reads in a 125.7M iteration loop is the ultimate performance anti-pattern. Even if it produced bit-exact results, the scattered load would obliterate any cycle savings from skipping the pop. It is mathematically destined to be a performance regression.

### 4. Bottom Line
**Conceded. Rule B beyond the shipped P-2 is dead.** 

Do not implement any multi-channel extensions of it. Your shipped P-2 hits the exact theoretical limit of what is topologically safe to prune on a turn-off event without re-walking. Rip out the multi-channel Rule B code and reclaim those cycles.
